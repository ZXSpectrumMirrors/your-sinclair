A Whole Newer Ball Game Dan Derham, 1999
Pacman. Now there's a game, eh? It changed popular culture. And what was it, really? Just a little face wandering about eating pills like it was going out of fashion. (I'll avoid the obvious jokes about Hollywood film stars.) It was a golden era, that. Pacman, Space Invaders, and of course Pong. Of course, that was ages ago; the kids today have no idea what Pacman is. They're all too busy playing such sophisticated games as Tomb Raider XXVII - Lara Goes Shopping. Blimmin' Eidos, and their capitalist system. (Sorry, kids - Nathan's off on another rant. Come back in a few minutes, and we'll see if he's finished, okay? Jon)
Anyway. A Whole New Ball Game was originally released in 1989, written by Pete Cooke. And now, to save me going to World of Spectrum and downloading a copy, we have Dan Derham's cunningly-titled remake to play with. It's written for Windows (oh no!) but luckily this month's Windows installation is working fine. Apart from... (He's off again. Jon) Alright, I won't make you suffer. AWNBG, as I've taken to calling it (even though it sounds like some kind of dangerous tropical insect: 'Watch out, Smithy! There's an Awnbug on the back of your chair!') also doesn't use the whole screen - it runs in its own (admittedly highly- ornate) window like a nice, well-behaved application should. It uses some of those standard Windows buttons, too, which makes getting a game started fairly easy.

Once the game is underway, you'll find yourself with a ball bouncing back and forth on the screen, a bunch of bright yellow energy pills arranged neatly (see, there was a reason for that Pacman rant) and some obstacles almost completely obstructing any path to them. Now, if this were Pacman, you could send the little ball careening into the pills with little difficulty, since there are no ghosts to chase you around and cause you grief. But this isn't Pacman. You don't control the ball, you control a little cursor, which you use to place mirrors to reflect the ball in the direction you want it to go. This is done with the mouse, which definitely makes things easier.
It's a simple concept, cleanly delivered. The graphics are sharp and simple, and the sound is minimalist (but then what do you expect, sampled speech?) It's very easy to get into, like all good puzzlers, and has a great difficulty curve, which draws you in at just the right pace before giving you some tricky new obstacles which make the time limit draw gradually tighter, like teleporters and a close cousin of the gooey blob from Chaos. Add a high score table (much overlooked these days) and you have a great little puzzler to compete for points at. Add in the fact that it uses a relatively small window, and it seems to me that it'd be perfect for playing on a PC at work.

Overall, it's a rather nice piece of work. As I said, perfect for the office PC, you can play it while looking to the casual observer as though you're working. The only real problem I had with it is that you have to click what item you want and then click where you want it every time - it doesn't remember what item you selected and then keep it until you change to another one, which gets quite frustrating later on. But on the other hand, the more you move the mouse about, the more it looks like you're doing something important.
Graphics: 55° Functional graphics, nice window border.
Sound: 30° Pong-like plinks and plunks, but hey, what can you do?
Life Expectancy: 65° With that high score table, AWNBG'll get pulled out every once in a while to try to top your personal best.
Gameplay: 65° Not bad, but can get a little repetitive.

Summary: A nice little puzzler, perfect for playing the odd game to beat your own score.

Nathan Cross
