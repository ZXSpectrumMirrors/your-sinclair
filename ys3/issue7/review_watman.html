Watman Kak, 2000
Hands up if you've seen Batman Forever? Crap, isn't it? Joel Schumacher took the skilfully-constructed environment conjured up from the depths of Tim Burton's mind and transformed it almost into a parody of itself. Introducing Robin was a move guaranteed to fail. Jim Carey's Riddler was too over-the-top, and Two-Face, a deeply complex character, was written off as just another bad guy. The sixties TV series was more enjoyable, because it didn't try to take itself seriously. And despite the cardboard sets, bad acting and funny camera angles, Adam West made a better Batman than Val Kilmer. And without any rubber, either.

Anyway, why am I ranting about Batman? Because (as if you hadn't already guessed) Watman is a remake of Batman. And not the programmed-by-numbers Batman the Movie, either; this is the original, honest-to-goodness isometric 3D Batman from the heady days of 1986. Written by _Kak_, this is a remake of the Ritman and Drummond original, and it's not bad at all.
If you've played the original, you'll be right at home here. This is probably the closest I've yet seen a remake get to the original material. If it weren't for a few (very few) tell-tale signs, you'd think it was the Warajevo emulator running compiled with a Batman snapshot. The sights, the sounds... it's just like the original, right down to the funny wibbly-wobbly noises when you're walking around.

But of course, if everything was just like the original there wouldn't be much point, would there? Probably the biggest difference has to be worked for - after playing for fifteen minutes (without getting killed!) the game presents you with a new option - colour. Yes, that's right, full colour. It looks a heck of a lot nicer in full colour, so I'd recommend that everyone who plays this game plays it for the fifteen minutes. (I cheated, using a stack of coins and some sellotape. Ahem. Not that I'm too crap to survive for fifteen minutes, or anything.)
As with the original, this is a very tricky game, and often quite frustrating, but it's coupled with an urge to see a bit further, just to the next room. Even the menu system is more difficult than a normal one (but that's more the fault of Jon Ritman, since the Watman menu system is identical). There's also the occasional (very occasional) glitch; I managed to restart stuck in the ceiling at one point. This one minor bug is the only thing that springs to mind from playing it, though; the quality overall is remarkable. It could be due to my personal experience with isometric engines (Stop him before he plugs his game! Jon), but I find the solidity of Watman and faithfulness to the original quite stunning.

Watman is great. The most accurate remake I've ever seen, and a playable game to boot. It's the Adam West to the Playstation's Batman games; tough, reliable, and no rubber. Ahem. We look forward to Isofarro's next remake, Head over Heels. If Watman is anything to go by, HoH will be amazing.
Graphics: 80° The Bernie Drummond originals... in glorious technicolour!
Sound: 30° People actually asked me to switch it off, but it's only as bad as the original.
Life Expectancy: 80° I've had this game in one form or another for about seven years, and I still haven't finished it.
Gameplay: 75° Frustrating at times, but generally an enjoyable adventure.

Summary: A solid and reliable remake. If you liked the original, then you like this too - it's identical, but with better graphics.

Nathan Cross
