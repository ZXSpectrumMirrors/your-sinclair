Jumping Jack's Back Craig Rothwell
You would think that after Neil Walker's Jumping Jack 2 (YS3 #6), and entering the Spectrum Tour 2000 (where Jumping Jack is one of the contest games), I'd be getting quite good at the game. And so I believed, until I played this game.

For those of you still unfamiliar with Jack, I'll explain a little. You play a man named Jack, whose sole ambition in life is to reach the top of a series of platforms. These platforms have holes in, through which Jack can jump to get to the next level. Of course, that in itself requires almost no skill whatsoever, and so just to make things a little bit challenging, the holes move. So if you're standing still on a platform, waiting for a hole to appear above you, inevitably one will soon slide underneath you and send you on what usually ends up as a spiral down to the ground floor, whereupon you lose a life.
Craig Rothwell is a name that should be familiar to most of you (since it's his name at the top of this page). He's the guy responsible (along with Matt Simmonds and Helen Foote) for a rather fine Jet Set Willy conversion, amongst lots of other things, and up until recently was working on a conversion of Head over Heels. According to the introductory scrolly, this is Craig's fifth remake. And it's none too shoddy, either.

The graphics are identical to the original. This was the first, and most unexpected thing to strike me about it. The second was the music. Typically of Mr Simmonds, it's really quite good (and playing in the background as I type this) and sets the tone of the game perfectly. Bouncy and happy music on the first couple of levels, quickly replaced by rather more menacing tunes later on.

Surprising even myself I hopped through to the fifth and final level on my first attempt. Admittedly I've had a lot of practice at Jumping Jack, but I'm still not that great. However, on the fifth level my luck ran out and my lives were guzzled up by repeated, infuriating falls to the ground. And so the pattern continued for several games after.
The most difficult thing about JJB is that it doesn't follow the same pattern as the original. While in the Spectrum version (and JJ2) you can see the holes coming as they cycle through the platforms above and below you, here they appear completely randomly at the end of each platform. There is no way you can predict what's coming, which introduces quite a large portion of blind luck into the game. To make up for this, though, there are no hazards of the kind that appear in the other two games (such as passing trains or Newcastle supporters). Your only enemies here are the holes.

Jumping Jack's Back is worth a look, if nothing else. In the end, though, I would have to recommend Jumping Jack 2 over it, not only because of the multiplayer options, but because despite the drastically different graphics, it is closer in gameplay to the original than the game which more closely resembles it.
Graphics: 20° It looks just like the original - a stick man and some platforms.
Sound: 70° Reasonable effects, and some great music by Matt Simmonds.
Life Expectancy: 50° Last level on my first go; I don't think it's going to stand up too much longer, either.
Gameplay: 80° It's Jumping Jack, but with less skill involved in playing it.

Summary: It's better than a lot of games out there, but I still prefer JJ2.

Nathan Cross
