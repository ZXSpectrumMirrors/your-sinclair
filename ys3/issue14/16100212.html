    Good day. I am Professor Winston K Pomegranate-Hugely, inventor of the motor-assisted confabulation device and the electric literature-enabling algorithm, a widely-respected scientific investigator. I have been invited by the charming editor of popular science-related magazine YS3 to draw your attention to a recent experiment, conducted by myself and my attractive-yet-routinely-ignored assistant Yolande Dorito, in an effort to further the scientific knowledge of the community.

    All music has suffered, at one time or another, from the view that exposure to it is discompositive to listeners' personalities. At one time even The Beatles were considered contumacious. Since its origins, rap music has suffered, and indeed benefited, from the popular perception of it as being less than morally copacetic. It is this view that this study aims to either confirm or disprove as we examine...

The Effect Of Long-Term Exposure To Rap Music

    The first problem we have before even beginning our study is in what we decide to classify as rap music. After all, many artists and fans could be grossly offended by our erroneous testing of rap music variants such as hip-hop, reggae and garage (and variants of each of these offshoots). It quickly became obvious that the only way to accurately determine the precise genre of any music used in our experiment would require a lengthy and complicated classification process to be engaged. Given the expense of such a study, and the rapidly-approaching deadline for this issue, we instead opted for the alternative offered by our editor ('Use these CDs or I'll have you buried in polystyrene.').
