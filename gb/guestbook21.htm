submitted by Mark Hemingway on Tue, 20 Apr 99



Until I found all the speccy nostalgia stuff on the net I used to feel like I was the only person in the world who got so nostalgic about it all. Loading up Sabre Wulf and hearing that beautiful title tune (a poor version of a prelude from Bach's "Well Tempered Clavier" I've since learned) takes me back to the early/mid-eighties again, a time when upgrading meant buying a joystick, when we stood gaping at arcade coin-ops like Outrun dreaming that maybe one day home computers might be able to have games as good as that, Battlestar Galactica ruled, & I used to argue with my Atari ST owning friends about whether Defender Of The Crown was as much fun as Chuckie Egg. I discovered a site where the person running it had undertaken the huge task of contacting all the old speccy software houses to get their permission to distribute the games, to avoid hassles of the fiends who try to shut down these emulation sites (maybe distributing N64 ROMS damages sales, but who can possibly be losing out by me downloading the speccy version of Ant Attack?) . He had tracked down a programmer of a few games published by one of the well known speccy software houses (can't remember which now), and the programmer had told him that the copyright of the games belonged to the owner of the software house, but not to worry about being pursued for breach of copyright as the guy now ran a company selling kitchens. It shattered all my illusions. To me the software houses of my childhood were vast monolithic organisations, employing hundreds of staff, with dozens of projects on the go at once. It's so sad to think that most of them were probably just small businesses run by people who have long ago lost all interest in the wonderful world of the speccy. Seeing that image from the last issue of YS of the 2 riders disappearing into the sunset, it brought tears to my eyes. I miss those days so much.
